# 📚 English Spelling & Grammar Rules: Simple Present Tense

This module focuses on the structural mechanics and contextual usage of the **Simple Present Tense**. This tense is primarily employed to communicate timeless facts, habitual routines, unchanging generalizations, and emotional states.

---

## 🔤 1. Base Sentence Structures

The formation of the simple present tense depends entirely on the subject of the sentence. Nouns and pronouns are divided into two primary conjugation groups:

### 🔹 Group A: Plural Subjects & First/Second Person (`I`, `You`, `We`, `They`)
When using plural subjects, the verb remains in its absolute base form (infinitive without "to").
* **Structure:** `Subject + Base Verb`
* *Example:* **Girls** love caring for injured animals.
* *Example:* **They** feed the neighborhood ducks every afternoon.

### 🔹 Group B: Third-Person Singular (`She`, `He`, `It`, Singular Nouns)
When the subject is a single person, girl, animal, or boy you must append an suffix (**-s** or **-es**) to the tail end of the base verb.
* **Structure:** `Subject + Verb(-s/-es)`
* *Example:* The **girl** feeds her parrot every morning.
* *Example:* The **puppy** barks loudly when the mail carrier arrives.

---

## 🔧 2. Third-Person Singular Spelling Shift Rules

When changing a base verb to fit a third-person singular subject (such as a single girl or a single animal), follow these strict spelling guidelines:

| Verb Ending Pattern | Applied Rule Shift | Project Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Most standard verbs** | Just add **-s** | *Run $\rightarrow$ Runs*<br>The **kitten runs** across the room. |
| **Ends in -ch, -sh, -ss, -x, -z, or -o** | Append **-es** | *Watch $\rightarrow$ Watches*<br>The **girl watches** the birds outside. |
| **Ends in Consonant + Y** | Drop the **-y**, add **-ies** | *Study $\rightarrow$ Studies*<br>The **girl studies** veterinary medicine. |
| **Ends in Vowel + Y** | Just add **-s** | *Play $\rightarrow$ Plays*<br>The **puppy plays** with its squeaky toy. |

## 🔄 3. Main Contextual Uses

The simple present tense is structurally selected for four primary real-world scenarios:

### 🔹 A. Habitual Actions and Routines
Actions that repeat regularly or follow a set schedule. These sentences often utilize frequency adverbs like *always, usually, or every day*.
* *Example:* The girl **wakes** up at 6:00 AM every single day to walk her dog.
* *Example:* The cat usually **sleeps** on the sunny windowsill during the afternoon.

### 🔹 B. Universal Facts and General Truths
Statements that are scientifically proven, timelessly true, or globally permanent.
* *Example:* **Water freezes** at $0^\circ\text{C}$ ($32^\circ\text{F}$).
* *Example:* A **cheetah runs** faster than any other land animal on earth.

### 🔹 C. Stative Verbs (Emotions, Thoughts, and Senses)
Verbs that describe a state of being, thoughts, or emotions rather than a physical action. These are rarely used in continuous (-ing) tenses.
* *Example:* The girl **knows** exactly how to train her new parakeet.
* *Example:* The kitten **wants** another bowl of warm milk.
