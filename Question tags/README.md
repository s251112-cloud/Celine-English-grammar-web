# English Grammar Guide: Question Tags 🗣️❓

Welcome to the ultimate guide on **Question Tags**! Question tags are the short questions added to the very end of a sentence (like *..., isn't it?* or *..., do you?*). 

We use them in conversational English for two main reasons:
1. To **confirm information** we think is true.
2. To **invite the other person to agree** with us.

---

## 1. The Golden Rule of Question Tags 🌟

The most important rule to remember is that question tags act like a mirror reflection. They opposite the tone of the main sentence:

*   **Rule 1:** If the main sentence is **Positive (+)**, the question tag must be **Negative (-)**.
*   **Rule 2:** If the main sentence is **Negative (-)**, the question tag must be **Positive (+)**.

| Main Sentence | Tag Tone | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Positive (+)** | **Negative (-)** | You are coming to the party, **aren't you**? |
| **Negative (-)** | **Positive (+)** | You aren't angry with me, **are you**? |

---

## 2. Choosing the Right Verb Tense

The verb in your question tag must match the tense of the main sentence. Here is how to handle different verb types:

### A. Sentences with the Verb "To Be"
If the sentence uses *am, is, are, was,* or *were*, repeat that exact same verb in the tag.
*   *Present:* She **is** an excellent student, **isn't she**?
*   *Past:* They **were** late for the GitHub workshop, **weren't they**?

### B. Sentences with Auxiliary / Modal Verbs
If the sentence contains a helping verb like *can, will, have, should,* or *must*, use that same verb in the tag.
*   *Example 1:* Celine **can** speak English fluently, **can't she**?
*   *Example 2:* We **won't** have homework over the weekend, **will we**?
*   *Example 3:* You **have** finished your grammar code, **haven't you**?

### C. Sentences WITHOUT a Helping Verb (Action Verbs)
If the sentence has a regular action verb (like *like, play, study, went*) and no helping verb, you must use **do, does, or did** in the tag!
*   *Present (Plural):* You **like** learning grammar, **don't you**?
*   *Present (Singular):* Celine **writes** beautiful web code, **doesn't she**?
*   *Past:* They **passed** the final English exam, **didn't they**?

---

## 3. Tricky Exceptions (Watch Out! 🚨)

English loves exceptions! Here are the special cases that often confuse students on tests:

### The "I am" Exception
You cannot say *"I am a coder, am n't I?"* because "am not I" doesn't exist as a contraction.
*   ❌ *Incorrect:* I am late, am not I?
*   ✅ **Correct:** I am late, **aren't I**? 
*   *(Note: If the sentence is negative, it stays normal: "I am not late, am I?")*

### Imperatives (Commands / Requests)
When you are asking someone to do something, use **will you** or **would you** to sound polite.
*   *Example:* Close the door, **will you**?
*   *Example:* Don't forget to commit your changes, **will you**?

### Let's (Suggestions)
When a sentence starts with "Let's" (Let us), the tag is always **shall we**.
*   *Example:* Let's practice our English conversation, **shall we**?

---

## 4. Intonation: How to Say Them 🔊

How you change the pitch of your voice at the end changes the entire meaning of the question tag:

1.  **Falling Intonation (Voice goes DOWN):** You are not really asking a question; you are just making conversation because you are 100% sure of the answer.
    *   *“It's a beautiful day, isn't it? ↘️”* (You know it's beautiful, you just want them to agree.)
2.  **Rising Intonation (Voice goes UP):** You genuinely do not know the answer and are checking to see if you are right.
    *   *“You didn't see my iPad, did you? ↗️”* (You are actually looking for your iPad and want an answer.)
